> **How to read these results**  
> - **Δθ (P−O)** is a *signed* difference (Person minus Organization). The sign indicates direction (which side is higher).  
> - **|Δθ|** is the *distance* between Person and Organization. **Closer to 0 = higher compatibility**; larger values mean greater mismatch.  
> - **Fit %** is an *interpretive index* derived from |Δθ| (capped) for quick scanning: **higher % = closer alignment**.  
> - Scores are standardized latent values (θ, z-scored). Use outputs as decision support, not as deterministic verdicts.

> **Methodological  note:** Fit % is an interpretive index derived from |Δθ| and linearly mapped to 0–100 using a cap (default **1.5**). Differences above the cap are truncated for stability. This improves visual interpretability; it is not a probability.

## Dimension-Level Results

### Goal Orientation

**Definition.** This dimension describes the extent to which an individual or organization prioritizes the achievement of final outcomes versus adherence to processes, methods, and means. From a cognitive perspective, it reflects the dominant attentional focus: whether it is directed toward the 'how' (means) or toward the 'what' (goals). It is not a value judgment: both poles can be functional depending on context, type of work, and the level of uncertainty.

**Compatibility diagnosis.**

- Person θ: **+1.32** → **Goal-Oriented**

- Organization θ: **+0.65** → **Goal-Oriented**

- Δθ (P−O): **+0.67** | |Δθ|: **0.67** | Fit %: **56%** → **Moderate-to-low compatibility**

**Cognitive attributes (both poles).**

- **Means-Oriented:**
  - *Attention to process and sequence*: Performance is mentally organized around steps, methods, and controls; procedural coherence serves as a key quality criterion.
  - *Evaluation via compliance and traceability*: Consistency of methods, documentation, and the ability to justify decisions through rules and procedures are highly valued.
  - *Preference for standardization*: Variability is reduced through repeatable practices; learning is integrated as an incremental improvement of the process.

- **Goal-Oriented:**
  - *Attention to outcomes and impact*: Decisions are anchored in goals, deliverables, and value; the process remains flexible if it improves outcomes.
  - *Evaluation via effectiveness*: Priority is given to whether actions achieve the objective; efficiency and goal attainment operate as central criteria.
  - *Goal-driven optimization*: There is a tendency to experiment, iterate, and adjust methods based on feedback about results.

**Observable cultural behaviors (both poles).**

- **Means-Oriented**

- Emphasis on standards, checklists, and explicitly defined steps.

- Expectation of documentation and traceability (decisions justified by procedure).

- Changes implemented in a controlled manner, with prior validation.

- **Goal-Oriented**
  - Priority on deliverables, goals, and impact metrics.
  - Tolerance for method flexibility if it accelerates outcomes.
  - Preference for rapid iterations and pragmatic, outcome-driven execution.

**Associated socioemotional skills (soft skills).**

- Means-Oriented: Rigor, operational discipline, organization, attention to detail, consistency.

- Goal-Oriented: Results orientation, proactivity, pragmatism, tactical adaptability, impact focus.

**Practical implications (moderate_low).**

- Frequent friction is likely in planning, estimation, and delivery pacing unless norms are clearly defined and reinforced.

- Higher risk of conflict around accountability: goals-first stakeholders may push speed, while process-first stakeholders push compliance and traceability.

- Without alignment, work may oscillate between rushed delivery and over-governance, reducing trust and perceived performance.

**Recommendations (moderate_low).**

**Onboarding recommendations**

- Implement structured onboarding with explicit workflow standards (checklists, sign-offs, review policies) or explicit outcome targets—depending on the org’s dominant pole.

- Schedule early 30/60/90-day alignment checkpoints focused on execution norms and friction patterns.

**Role design recommendations**

- Avoid high-ambiguity roles unless the org provides strong scaffolding (process) or strong outcome guardrails (metrics).

- Consider pairing with a complementary counterpart (goal-driven + process-driven) to stabilize delivery.

**Coaching / training recommendations**

- Provide structured coaching on either process discipline (documentation, compliance) or outcome thinking (impact, prioritization), depending on the misfit direction.

- Use real examples of conflicts to build shared mental models.

**Risks to monitor**

- Watch for rework cycles, delayed approvals, and blame patterns around process vs outcomes.

### Internal vs external motivation

**Definition.** This dimension captures the predominant source of work motivation. It distinguishes between contexts in which action is regulated mainly by external contingencies (rewards, recognition, pressure, supervision) and contexts in which internalized or intrinsic motivation predominates (interest, personal values, purpose, and autonomy). At a cognitive level, it influences persistence, commitment, and how feedback is interpreted.

**Compatibility diagnosis.**

- Person θ: **+1.44** → **Internally driven**

- Organization θ: **+0.10** → **Internally driven**

- Δθ (P−O): **+1.34** | |Δθ|: **1.34** | Fit %: **11%** → **Low compatibility**

**Cognitive attributes (both poles).**

- **External motivation:**
  - *Regulation by contingencies*: Behavior is organized around rewards, sanctions, or external expectations; incentives guide prioritization.
  - *Feedback as external validation*: Feedback is processed as a signal of approval/acceptance; social recognition carries substantial weight.
  - *Preference for structure and direction*: Clear external guidance (imposed goals, supervision, KPIs) is valued to support effort and persistence.

- **Internal motivation:**
  - *Autonomy and meaning*: Behavior is guided by values, purpose, and choice; effort is sustained even without immediate incentives.
  - *Feedback as information to improve*: Feedback is processed as learning input rather than judgment; mastery and growth are prioritized.
  - *Intrinsic persistence*: Commitment is maintained through interest and meaning; motivation remains more stable under reward changes.

**Observable cultural behaviors (both poles).**

- **External motivation**

- Effort increases in response to rewards, bonuses, recognition, or external pressure.

- Frequent confirmation and clear external evaluation are sought.

- Tasks that are highly visible or directly rewarded tend to be prioritized.

- **Internal motivation**
  - Effort is sustained through interest, meaning, and learning.
  - Autonomy and ownership are actively sought.
  - Mastery/competence and continuous improvement are valued as motivators.

**Associated socioemotional skills (soft skills).**

- External motivation: compliance orientation, responsiveness to external feedback, adaptability to supervision, incentive-driven pragmatism.

- Internal motivation: autonomy, sustained motivation, resilience, self-directed learning, and intrinsic responsibility.

**Practical implications (low).**

- Strong mismatch in motivational regulation: sustained effort may be hard to maintain under the organization’s dominant incentives/controls.

- High risk of chronic disengagement, misread feedback (judgment vs information), and attrition.

- This should be treated as a primary fit risk requiring structured intervention or role re-design.

**Recommendations (low).**

**Onboarding recommendations**

- Treat as critical onboarding risk: define a motivational contract (autonomy, rewards, supervision style, growth path).

- Assign a mentor/manager style match; increase feedback frequency and monitor early disengagement signals.

**Role design recommendations**

- Avoid roles where sustained engagement is essential without strong motivational scaffolding.

- If unavoidable, redesign incentives and supervision style to prevent repeated disengagement cycles.

**Coaching / training recommendations**

- High-touch coaching + manager training is required; misfit rarely self-corrects.

- If repeated disengagement persists, consider role change or different incentive structure.

**Risks to monitor**

- Watch for chronic dissatisfaction, avoidance, performance instability, and early attrition signals—intervene fast.

**Evidence Pack (light).**

- **Inferred pole:** Internally Driven

- **Confidence:** 0.90

- **Notes:** Lexicon low hits: LLM weighted more

**Key evidence signals**

- me mueve la satisfacción de ver que lo que construyo funciona

- me gusta tener autonomía y que confíen en mi criterio profesional

### Flexibility vs structure

**Definition.** This dimension describes a preference for flexible, adaptive, ambiguity-tolerant environments versus normative, structured, and highly regulated environments. Cognitively, it reflects the role of rules as context-dependent guides versus stable and obligatory frameworks. It influences how exceptions are managed, how rules are interpreted, and how organizational control is perceived.

**Compatibility diagnosis.**

- Person θ: **-1.09** → **Flexible (Easygoing)**

- Organization θ: **-0.10** → **Flexible (Easygoing)**

- Δθ (P−O): **-0.99** | |Δθ|: **0.99** | Fit %: **34%** → **Low compatibility**

**Cognitive attributes (both poles).**

- **Flexibility:**
  - *Tolerance for ambiguity*: Exceptions are processed as a normal part of work; the framework is adjusted according to context.
  - *Rules as flexible guidelines*: Norms are interpreted situationally; adaptability and judgment are prioritized.
  - *Preference for contextual autonomy*: Room to decide and experiment is valued without excessive formality.

- **Structure:**
  - *Preference for predictability*: Uncertainty is reduced through clear rules; consistency and stability are valued.
  - *Rules as standards*: Norms are interpreted literally and generalizable; individual variation is minimized.
  - *Control and compliance*: Compliance, auditing, and traceability are prioritized; exceptions require formal justification.

**Observable cultural behaviors (both poles).**

- **Flexibility**

- Rapid and pragmatic changes in response to new circumstances.

- Exceptions are accepted if they add value or remove blockers.

- Preference for informal agreements and agile coordination.

- **Structure**
  - Strict compliance with policies, processes, and standards.
  - Exceptions require approval, evidence, or documentation.
  - Preference for routines, defined roles, and stable procedures.

**Associated socioemotional skills (soft skills).**

- Flexibility: adaptability, creativity, cognitive flexibility, tolerance for change, initiative.

- Structure: discipline, reliability, organization, self-control, and operational consistency.

**Practical implications (low).**

- High mismatch in rule interpretation and uncertainty handling; coordination overhead and rework are likely persistent.

- Major risk of conflict over compliance, approvals, and autonomy boundaries.

- Treat as a primary fit risk: requires explicit governance design or role reassignment.

**Recommendations (low).**

**Onboarding recommendations**

- Treat as critical onboarding risk: enforce clear rules or explicitly grant autonomy with guardrails—do not leave norms implicit.

- Assign a process owner/mentor to ensure stability and reduce repeated conflict.

**Role design recommendations**

- Avoid roles requiring constant negotiation of governance; mismatch will be costly.

- If unavoidable, formalize approval workflows and exception documentation.

**Coaching / training recommendations**

- High-touch coaching + structural interventions required; misfit rarely self-corrects.

- If repeated conflicts persist, consider role redesign or different team placement.

**Risks to monitor**

- Watch for chronic conflict, blocked delivery, and repeated escalation; intervene early with clear governance rules.

### Open vs Closed System

**Definition.** This dimension reflects the extent to which an individual or an organization orient toward the external environment (customers, market, stakeholders, external feedback) versus an orientation centered on internal processes, stability, and boundary control. It regulates collective attention: which signals are considered relevant and how information is prioritized for decision-making.

**Compatibility diagnosis.**

- Person θ: **+1.39** → **Open System**

- Organization θ: **+0.90** → **Open System**

- Δθ (P−O): **+0.49** | |Δθ|: **0.49** | Fit %: **67%** → **Moderate compatibility**

**Cognitive attributes (both poles).**

- **Closed System:**
  - *Protection of internal coherence*: Stability, control, and consistency are prioritized; the environment is filtered to avoid noise or drift.
  - *Attention to internal rules*: Decisions are grounded in internal standards, processes, and institutional priorities.
  - *Preference for clear boundaries*: A separation between 'inside' and 'outside' is maintained; external change is adopted cautiously.

- **Open System:**
  - *Sensitivity to external signals*: Customer/market information is prioritized; the environment guides adaptation and innovation.
  - *Learning through feedback*: External feedback is integrated as a central input to improve processes and products.
  - *Adaptive openness*: Reconfiguration of internal practices is tolerated to respond to external demands.

**Observable cultural behaviors (both poles).**

- **Closed System**

- Emphasis on internal procedures and operational stability.

- External changes are adopted slowly and with formal filters.

- Preference for information control and internal prioritization.

- **Open System**
  - Frequent customer/market contact and continuous learning.
  - Iterative adjustments based on external feedback.
  - Active exploration of trends and external benchmarking.

**Associated socioemotional skills (soft skills).**

- Closed System: reliability, internal focus, procedural thinking, prudence, consistency.

- Open System: customer orientation, curiosity, strategic adaptability, openness to learning, initiative.

**Practical implications (moderate).**

- Differences in ‘signal relevance’ may cause repeated debates: adapt to external feedback vs protect internal coherence.

- Risk: one side perceives the other as ‘too reactive’ or ‘too insular’.

- Alignment improves by defining how external inputs enter the decision pipeline.

**Recommendations (moderate).**

**Onboarding recommendations**

- Define a decision protocol: when external feedback triggers change vs when internal stability wins.

- Create clear stakeholder communication norms to avoid ‘boundary confusion’.

**Role design recommendations**

- Prefer roles with explicit interfaces: clear channel for external inputs + clear internal constraints.

- Reduce ambiguity by clarifying who can commit to external stakeholders.

**Coaching / training recommendations**

- Coach on reframing: openness ≠ chaos, closed ≠ stagnation; both manage risk differently.

- Train teams on structured feedback loops (intake → triage → decision → communicate).

**Risks to monitor**

- Watch for recurring arguments about responsiveness, ‘scope creep’, and whether external signals are trusted.

**Evidence Pack (light).**

- **Inferred pole:** Open System

- **Confidence:** 0.88

- **Notes:** Lexicon low hits: LLM weighted more

### People vs task orientation

**Definition.** This dimension differentiates cultures that prioritize well-being, human relationships, and socioemotional climate versus those that prioritize efficiency, performance, and task execution. Cognitively, it regulates how success is defined: by human impact and team care versus productive output and work results.

**Compatibility diagnosis.**

- Person θ: **-0.98** → **People-oriented (Employee-Oriented)**

- Organization θ: **+0.40** → **Task-oriented (Job-Oriented)**

- Δθ (P−O): **-1.38** | |Δθ|: **1.38** | Fit %: **8%** → **Low compatibility**

**Cognitive attributes (both poles).**

- **People:**
  - *Primacy of well-being and relationship*: Decisions consider human impact; psychological safety and climate are valued.
  - *Success as social sustainability*: Effectiveness includes cohesion, support, and development; the process matters due to interpersonal impact.
  - *Attention to socioemotional signals*: Tension, workload, and motivation are monitored; coordination is adjusted to sustain the team.

- **Tasks:**
  - *Primacy of performance and output*: Delivery, efficiency, and attainment are prioritized; resources are organized toward results.
  - *Success as execution*: Effectiveness is defined by observable performance, productivity, and goals; well-being is managed as a facilitator.
  - *Attention to task metrics*: Progress, blockers, and work quality are monitored; coordination is oriented to throughput.

**Observable cultural behaviors (both poles).**

- **People**

- Feedback emphasizes care, support, and personal development.

- Decisions prioritize balance, climate, and team sustainability.

- A culture of collaboration and interpersonal support.

- **Tasks**
  - Feedback is oriented to metrics, performance, and results.
  - Decisions are guided by efficiency, priorities, and execution.
  - A culture of accountability and deliverable focus.

**Associated socioemotional skills (soft skills).**

- People: empathy, support, caring communication, interpersonal sensitivity, collaboration.

- Tasks: assertiveness, time management, accountability, focus, prioritization, results orientation.

**Practical implications (low).**

- High mismatch in socioemotional climate vs execution expectations; conflict and dissatisfaction are likely persistent.

- Strong risk of disengagement, feedback breakdown, and repeated interpersonal friction.

- Treat as a primary fit risk; requires coaching, role design, and communication governance.

**Recommendations (low).**

**Onboarding recommendations**

- Treat as critical onboarding risk: establish explicit feedback rules and conflict-resolution protocol.

- Assign a mentor or manager to calibrate expectations and protect psychological safety.

**Role design recommendations**

- Avoid roles where feedback and collaboration intensity is high without strong support.

- If unavoidable, redesign responsibilities and install robust communication scaffolding.

**Coaching / training recommendations**

- High-touch coaching required; misfit rarely self-corrects.

- If repeated friction persists, consider role change or different team placement.

**Risks to monitor**

- Watch for chronic interpersonal conflict, disengagement, and high turnover intent; intervene early.

### Professional identity

**Definition.** This dimension describes the primary locus of work identity: whether the professional self is anchored mainly in the specific organization one belongs to (parochial) or in the profession, expertise, and external standards (professional). Cognitively, it influences success criteria, loyalty, development, and ethical decision frameworks.

**Compatibility diagnosis.**

- Person θ: **+1.49** → **Professional identity (Professional)**

- Organization θ: **+0.30** → **Professional identity (Professional)**

- Δθ (P−O): **+1.19** | |Δθ|: **1.19** | Fit %: **21%** → **Low compatibility**

**Cognitive attributes (both poles).**

- **Parochial:**
  - *Work-self as belonging*: Identity is represented as part of an organizational 'we'; affiliation and internal cultural continuity take priority.
  - *Internal success criteria*: Alignment with internal norms, institutional expectations, and recognition within the organization is valued.
  - *Decisions anchored in internal context*: Organization-defined priorities are emphasized; external standards are subordinated to local culture.

- **Professional:**
  - *Work-self as expertise*: Identity is represented around the profession, technical standards, and external disciplinary communities.
  - *External quality criteria*: Alignment with best practices, industry standards, and professional reputation is valued.
  - *Decisions anchored in professional principles*: Technical consistency and professional ethics are prioritized; internal culture is evaluated against external frameworks.

**Observable cultural behaviors (both poles).**

- **Parochial**

- Loyalty and commitment centered on the organization and its culture.

- Preference for internal standards and 'how we do things here'.

- Internal recognition as the primary validator of career trajectory.

- **Professional**
  - Loyalty and commitment centered on the profession and external standards.
  - Use of external benchmarking and best practices as reference.
  - External professional recognition (certifications, community) as a relevant validator.

**Associated socioemotional skills (soft skills).**

- Parochial: affiliation, internal cultural adaptability, belonging-building, contextual cooperation.

- Professional: technical judgment, professional ethics, expertise-based autonomy, continuous disciplinary learning.

**Practical implications (low).**

- High mismatch in identity locus: conflict about legitimacy, standards, and loyalty is likely persistent.

- Strong risk of repeated friction in decision-making and performance evaluation criteria.

- Treat as a primary fit risk; requires explicit standards governance, mentoring, or role change.

**Recommendations (low).**

**Onboarding recommendations**

- Assign a mentor who can translate between internal culture and external professional expectations.

**Role design recommendations**

- Avoid roles requiring high identity alignment (leadership, culture shaping) if mismatch is large.

- If unavoidable, formalize standards governance and recognition criteria.

**Coaching / training recommendations**

- High-touch coaching required; misfit rarely self-corrects.

- If persistent, consider role reassignment to reduce identity friction exposure.

**Risks to monitor**

- Watch for chronic conflict, disengagement, and stalled decisions; intervene early with governance.
